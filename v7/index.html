02 The Lede

CHICAGO — March 2026

It starts with a name you don’t recognize, an email from a domain you’ve never seen in your inbox, and a suggestion to meet at a coffee shop you’ve walked past a hundred times but never entered. This is Campus Coffee Roulette, and in less than two academic years, it has quietly become the most improbable networking phenomenon in Chicago’s graduate ecosystem.

The premise is disarmingly simple. You sign up. An algorithm matches you with a graduate student from a different program, a different school, a different intellectual universe. You get a name, an email, and a week to find thirty minutes for coffee. That’s it. No icebreaker prompts. No LinkedIn profiles. No agenda.

“We wanted to build something that felt like the opposite of a networking event.”
— Founding team member

What happens next is the interesting part. A computational biology Ph.D. candidate at UChicago sits across from a social work student at Loyola. A mechanical engineering researcher at IIT discovers that the poet from Northwestern she’s been matched with is working on the same fundamental question about pattern recognition—just in a wildly different language.

The project began in the autumn of 2024, when a small group of graduate students noticed something troubling: Chicago has one of the densest concentrations of graduate programs in the country, yet students at neighboring institutions might as well be on different continents. The silos were total. The isolation was chronic.1

“You could spend five years getting a Ph.D. at UChicago and never once have a real conversation with someone at Northwestern or IIT,” recalls one early organizer. “That struck us as absurd. These are people solving pieces of the same puzzles, and they don’t even know each other exist.”

Coffee Roulette was designed as an intervention—but a gentle one. No mandatory fun. No forced collaboration. Just an introduction, a location, and the radical proposition that conversation between strangers might still be worth something in an age of algorithmic feeds and professional optimization.

03 The Mechanism

How the
Machine
Works

01

Registration

You provide a name, a university email, your institution, and your program. Nothing else. No bios, no headshots, no list of interests. The less information, the more serendipity.

02

The Match

Each Monday, the system pairs you with someone from a different program—ideally a different school entirely. You receive an email with their name and contact. They receive yours.

03

The Meeting

You have seven days. Find a coffee shop. Find thirty minutes. Show up as yourself. There are no discussion guides, no reporting requirements, no follow-up surveys.

04

The Ripple

Some matches become collaborators. Some become friends. Some remain a single, pleasant conversation. All of them crack the shell of institutional isolation, even if only by a fraction.

“The algorithm is deliberately stupid. It doesn’t try to find your soulmate. It just finds someone different.”

1 A 2024 survey by the Chicago Graduate Student Alliance found that 73% of respondents had never had a substantive conversation with a graduate student at another Chicago institution during their entire program.

04 The Landscape

Seven Schools.
One City.

Chicago’s graduate landscape is both a marvel and a paradox. Within a thirty-mile radius, seven major institutions house thousands of graduate students pursuing hundreds of distinct programs. They ride the same L trains, frequent the same coffee shops, and wrestle with the same existential questions about their futures—yet they inhabit parallel universes.

Hyde Park

University of Chicago

Where interdisciplinary was a founding principle, not a buzzword. Home to 10,000+ graduate and professional students across the sciences, humanities, law, business, and public policy.

Evanston / Chicago

Northwestern University

Straddling the city-suburb divide with programs spanning engineering, journalism, performing arts, and medicine. A campus that looks toward the lake and, increasingly, toward the city.

Near West Side

University of Illinois Chicago

The city’s public research powerhouse. UIC’s graduate programs in urban planning, public health, and education are shaped by the city that surrounds them.

Rogers Park / Water Tower

Loyola University Chicago

A Jesuit institution with deep roots in social justice. Graduate programs in social work, psychology, and bioethics draw students committed to community impact.

Lincoln Park / Loop

DePaul University

The largest Catholic university in the nation, with graduate strengths in computer science, education, business, and the performing arts.

Bronzeville

Illinois Institute of Technology

Mies van der Rohe’s campus is a fitting home for programs in architecture, engineering, computer science, and design that emphasize building the future.

Across the City

& Others

SAIC, Columbia College, Adler University, and more. Chicago’s graduate ecosystem extends far beyond the usual suspects. All are welcome.

06 Voices

What They
Found

Maria Chen was three years into her Ph.D. in astrophysics at UChicago when she was matched with a design student at IIT. “I hadn’t spoken to anyone outside my department in months,” she says. “Not because I didn’t want to. Because the infrastructure for it simply didn’t exist.” That single coffee led to a data visualization collaboration that was later presented at a national conference.

“I signed up on a whim. It was the best professional decision I made in graduate school.”
— M.A. candidate, Northwestern

James Okafor, a public health student at UIC, puts it differently. “Grad school is lonely in a way that nobody warns you about. Coffee Roulette didn’t solve that, but it reminded me that there are thousands of people in this city going through the exact same thing. That mattered.”

Not every match produces a revelation. Some conversations are pleasant but unremarkable. The organizers consider this a feature, not a bug. “We’re not optimizing for outcomes,” one explains. “We’re optimizing for encounters. The outcomes take care of themselves.”
